Pakistan

Pakistan represents a rich and diverse cultural heritage that reflects history and influences from various civilizations. From the vibrant traditions of classical music, such as Qawwali, to the intricate artistry of traditional crafts like pottery and handwoven textiles, Pakistani culture is a tapestry woven with the threads of multiple ethnicities.

The festivals of the country, such as Eid and Basant, showcase the warmth, hospitality, and deep-rooted cultural values that define the mosaic of Pakistani society.

FLAVOURS OF PAKISTAN

Nihari-National dish of Pakistan

"Nihari, comes from the Arabic word "nahaar" which means "morning," signifies its historical purpose as a delightful morning meal, offering a flavorful and fresh start to your day.
Originally nihari was made with beef shank, but in recent times lamb, mutton and chicken have also been substituted successfully. The addition of maghaz (brain) and nalli (bone marrow) is also loved and considered a delicacy. Of course, each variant has different cooking times according to the protein which has been used. For example, chicken requires much less time in comparison to beef shanks.





WEEDING CULTURE OF PAKISTAN

A Pakistani wedding is a vibrant and intricate celebration that reflects the rich cultural tapestry of the country. Typically extending several days, the festivities encompass a series of traditional ceremonies and rituals, each marked by music, dance, sumptuous feasts and lavish banquets. The "Mehndi" ceremony kicks off the celebrations, where ornate henna designs are applied to the bride's hands and feet, accompanied by lively music, spirited melodies and ryhthmic dances. The "Baraat", a majestic procession led by the groom, is a highlight, culminating in the "Nikah", the formal wedding ceremony. The "Walima", a reception hosted by the groom's family, concludes the festivities. The weddings are characterized by colorful attire, intricate jewelry, and a strong emphasis on family and community participation. The cultural diversity within Pakistan adds further nuances to wedding traditions, resulting in a joyous and memorable union of two families.




Top sight in Pakistan

Badshahi Mosque

The Badshahi Mosque, situated in Lahore, Pakistan. Commissioned by the sixth Mughal Emperor Aurangzeb in 1671 and finalized in 1673, the mosque emerge as one of the largest and most magnificent mosques in the world. Known as, "Badshahi," translates to "Imperial," reflecting its regal significance. The mosque's design is a showcase to Mughal architectural brilliance, characterized by red sandstone and white marble embellishments, intricate calligraphy, and delicate inlay craftsmanship. The grand courtyard and the main prayer hall, capable of accommodating thousands of worshippers, epitomize the mosque's grandeur and size. The iconic structure is adorned with four lofty minarets, each offering a stunning view of the surrounding landscape. Over the centuries, the Badshahi Mosque has endured as a symbol of cultural heritage, attracting visitors and worshippers alike, and continues to stand as a majestic representation of Pakistan's rich historical lagacy and architectural mastery.
